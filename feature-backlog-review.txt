Ordered HARDEST -> EASIEST. Difficulty is rated for this codebase
specifically: a single static index.html, no backend, localStorage,

improves the app and its users.

============================================================
HARDEST — fights the architecture or the platform
============================================================

1. Native app wrapper (Capacitor/Tauri shell around the PWA)
   What: wrap the existing index.html in a thin native iOS/Android
   shell. The web app stays the single source of truth.
   Why it's hardest: new toolchain, signing, App Store review, and a
   second distribution channel to maintain — everything the
   "one static file" philosophy avoids.
   How it improves the app: it converts three permanent PWA
   weaknesses into solved problems at once — guaranteed rest-timer
   notifications even on iPhone, real haptics instead of
   navigator.vibrate (ignored by iOS Safari), and HealthKit/Google
   Fit access. If GainPath ever feels limited, this is the single
   move that lifts the ceiling; nothing else on this list does.

2. Cloud backup & sync (accounts)
   What: user accounts with automatic history sync across devices.
   Why it's hardest: needs a real backend — hosting, auth, privacy
   obligations. Directly against the "your data stays on your
   device" design.
   How it improves the app: today one cleared Safari cache or lost
   phone erases months of training history unless the user manually
   exported. Sync makes data loss structurally impossible and
   enables phone+iPad use. Honest counterweight: item 12 (backup
   nudges) captures ~80% of this value at ~2% of the cost, so this
   should only happen if GainPath ever grows beyond personal use.

3. Apple Health / Google Fit integration
   What: write completed workouts (type, duration, volume) to the
   phone's health store.
   Why it's hard: effectively blocked while GainPath is a pure PWA —
   requires item 1 first.
   How it improves the app: GainPath sessions would count toward
   activity rings/Move goals, and coexist with whatever else the
   user tracks (runs, weight from a smart scale). It makes GainPath
   a citizen of the user's health ecosystem instead of an island.

============================================================
HARD — big subsystems, but fully doable in-file
============================================================

4. Progress photos
   What: periodic physique photos attached to weigh-ins, with a
   side-by-side compare view.
   Why it's hard: photos blow past localStorage limits immediately —
   needs IndexedDB, image downscaling/compression, and storage-
   pressure handling; also must be excluded from JSON export or the
   backup file becomes enormous.
   How it improves the app: strength numbers lag visual change;
   photos are the motivation layer numbers can't provide. For a
   "definition" goal user (an onboarding option!) this is arguably
   the missing core feature.

5. Deload & overtraining advisor (deterministic, not "AI")
   What: watch the per-exercise feel ratings and volume trend the
   app already stores; when several sessions trend "Hard"/"Too
   much" or e1RM stalls 3+ sessions, suggest a deload week (e.g.
   "-30% weights this week?") as an Apply/Dismiss chip — same
   consent pattern as the existing weight suggestions.
   Why it's hard: the detection rules need care to avoid nagging;
   touches suggestion logic, session generation, and copy. Must
   stay a transparent formula (per project rules: never branded AI).
   How it improves the app: beginners don't know when to back off —
   they stall, get beat up, and quit. The app already collects the
   exact data needed; this turns it into the coaching moment with
   the highest real-world value.

6. Custom exercises
   What: user-defined exercises (name, muscle group, equipment)
   folded into the picker, with YouTube-search fallback instead of
   a bundled image.
   Why it's hard: EXPOOL is currently a fixed constant; custom
   entries must survive in every consumer (PRs, charts, suggestions,
   export/import, image lookup) without the guaranteed image the
   image-naming rule assumes.
   How it improves the app: no database covers every gym. One
   missing machine currently means the user's real training silently
   doesn't fit the app; this removes the last "sorry, can't log
   that" wall.

7. Weekly muscle-group volume balance
   What: a Progress-tab view aggregating weekly hard sets per muscle
   group from the mg tag every exercise already has ("Chest 14 sets ·
   Back 6 sets — imbalance").
   Why it's hard(ish): new aggregation + a new chart and the "what's
   balanced?" reference ranges; pure computation, no data-model
   change.
   How it improves the app: the classic self-taught failure mode is
   invisible imbalance (mirror muscles get everything). This makes
   it visible in one glance and quietly teaches programming sense —
   strong differentiator for a free tracker.

8. Drop sets & rest-pause sets
   What: intensity techniques on the last set (weight drops within
   one set; short-rest continuation sets), configured per exercise.
   Why it's hard(ish): supersets shipped in v1.4, but these change
   what "a set" is — set rendering, rest-timer behavior, volume math
   and PR logic all need a sub-set concept.
   How it improves the app: intermediate/advanced users use these
   weekly; today they either fake-log them (corrupting volume/PR
   data) or don't log them. Closes the last gap between "what I did"
   and "what I could record".

============================================================
MEDIUM — clear wins, contained scope
============================================================

9. Plate & dumbbell inventory ("my gym")
   What: let the user record available plates (pairs) and the
   dumbbell rack increments; the plate calculator then only shows
   loadable combinations, and weight suggestions round to weights
   that physically exist in their gym.
   How it improves the app: kills the most common friction moment —
   being told "try 62.5 kg" when the rack jumps 60→65. Suggestions
   go from theoretically right to actually usable, which directly
   increases how often users tap Apply.

10. Shareable session summary card
    What: after "Session done!", render a clean PNG (canvas) of the
    summary — day, duration, volume, PRs — via the Web Share API /
    long-press save.
    How it improves the app: users already screenshot summaries to
    send to friends. A designed card makes the habit effortless and
    is the only realistic growth loop a no-backend app has — every
    share is an ad with zero infrastructure.

11. In-app hold timer for timed exercises
    What: for timed holds (Plank, Dead hang, Side plank if added), a
    live count-up/target countdown inside the set row with the same
    beeps as the rest timer, instead of eyeballing a wall clock.
    How it improves the app: timed exercises are currently the only
    movements the app can't actively guide through. One less reason
    to reach for a separate stopwatch mid-workout.

12. Backup health nudges
    What: track sessions-since-last-export; after N sessions or M
    weeks, show a small dismissible home banner ("32 sessions since
    your last backup — Export now?"), one tap to the existing
    export.
    How it improves the app: localStorage is one aggressive "clear
    browsing data" away from gone. This is the cheap insurance
    version of feature 2 — it can't prevent loss, but it makes
    "I lost everything and my last backup was yesterday" the normal
    case. Highest value-per-effort in the Medium tier.

13. Per-session & per-exercise notes
    What: a small free-text note on any exercise during logging
    ("left shoulder pinch", "seat position 4") and on the session,
    shown in history and on the next visit to that exercise.
    How it improves the app: machine seat settings and pain flags
    are exactly what lifters forget between sessions. The next-visit
    resurfacing turns notes from a diary into a memory prosthetic.

============================================================
EASY — small, self-contained, high satisfaction
============================================================

14. Keep-screen-awake during workouts (Wake Lock API)
    What: a settings toggle; request a screen wake lock while a
    session or rest timer is active.
    How it improves the app: the #1 mid-workout annoyance — the
    screen locking between sets, forcing FaceID/PIN with chalky
    hands every 90 seconds. ~20 lines including the toggle;
    supported in iOS 16.4+/Android Chrome.

15. Quick ±2.5 steppers on weight inputs
    What: small −/+ buttons beside each weight input during logging
    (step = 2.5 kg / 5 lbs, or 1 kg for dumbbells under 20).
    How it improves the app: typing numbers on a phone keyboard
    mid-set is the clumsiest interaction in the whole flow; ~90% of
    weight changes are one increment up or down. Two taps replace
    keyboard entry almost everywhere.

16. Streak shield / planned rest weeks
    What: mark a week as planned rest (holiday, illness, deload):
    the streak pauses instead of resetting to zero.
    How it improves the app: a streak that dies during a flu or a
    family trip punishes life, not laziness — and a dead long streak
    is the single most demotivating thing a habit tracker can do.
    One flag in the streak() walk fixes it.

17. CSV export
    What: alongside JSON backup and PDF report, a flat CSV of all
    sets (date, day, exercise, set, weight, reps, feel).
    How it improves the app: JSON is for machines, PDF is for
    reading — CSV is for the spreadsheet crowd (and Jed's own
    Financial-Command-Center instincts). Power users get analysis
    freedom without GainPath having to build every chart they might
    ever want.

18. PR history timeline per exercise
    What: on the PR list, tapping an exercise shows its full PR
    progression (every time the record moved, not just the current
    best) as a mini timeline.
    How it improves the app: "current PR" says where you are; the
    trail of broken records says how far you've come — cheap,
    data-already-exists motivation on the app's proudest screen.
